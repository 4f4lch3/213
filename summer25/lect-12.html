Point Estimation

A point estimate is a single number that is based on sample data and represents a plausible (estimate) value of a population characteristic.

Name Parameter Statistic
Mean \(\mu\) \(\bar{X}\)
Variance \(\sigma^2\) \(S^2\)
Standard Deviation \(\sigma\) S
Proportion p \(\hat{p}\)
Total N \(\hat{N}\)

  • The statistic is a point estimator of a parameter.

  • The goal of the statistical inference is to use the information obtained from the sample and generalize the results to the population being studied.

  • IMPORTANT: The “sample” MUST BE a good representation of the population.

  • We use “estimation” techniques to assess possible values (or value) that are close to (or include) the population characteristics (parameter).

  • A statistic is unbiased if its mean value is equal to the value of the population characteristic being studied. Otherwise, the statistic is biased.

  • Given several unbiased statistics that could be used for estimating a population characteristic, the best choice is the statistic with the smallest standard deviation.

You select a random sample of 180 people at a medical convention attended by 1,880 people. Within your sample, you find that 64 people have traveled from abroad.

a) Based on this sample statistic, estimate how many people at the convention traveled from abroad.

  • Although the question asks for the total in the population, it is necessary to “estimate” the proportion in the population.

  • The sample proportion, \(\hat{p}\), is the point estimator of p.

  • \(\hat{p}\) = \(\frac{64}{180}\) = 0.3556

  • We estimate that 35.56% of ALL people in the medical convention traveled from abroad.

  • In order to estimate the total of people who traveled from abroad, we need to use the point estimate, \(\hat{p}\).

  • \(\hat{N}\) = \(\hat{p}\)N = (0.3556)(1,880) = 668.528

  • We estimate that about 668 people at the convention traveled from abroad.

b) Would you be more confident of your estimate if you sampled 300 people?

  • Yes

  • More data imply more information about the population characteristics (parameters and distribution).

Confidence Intervals (Definition)

  • A confidence interval for an unknown parameter consists of an interval of numbers based on the point estimate.

  • Confidence Interval: Point Estimate \(\pm\) Margin of Error.

  • A confidence level is the success rate of the method used to construct a confidence interval.

  • The purpose of using an “interval estimator” is to have some degree of confidence of securing the true parameter.

Large Sample Confidence Interval for a Population Proportion

  • When n is large, a 95% confidence interval for p is

  • \(\hat{p} - 1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p})}{n}}, \hat{p} + 1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p})}{n}}\); where \(\hat{p}\) is the sample proportion.

  • Abbreviated formula: \(\hat{p} \pm 1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p})}{n}}\)

  • The interval can be used as long as:

    1. \(n\hat{p} \geq 10\) and \(n(1 - \hat{p}) \geq 10\)

    2. The sample size is less than 10% of the population size if sampling is without replacement.

    3. The sample can be regarded as a random sample from the population of interest.

  • The expression \(1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p})}{n}}\) is the margin of error for 95% confidence level.

  • Other commonly used confidence levels, 90% and 99%, use Z critical values 1.645 and 2.58 respectively. See textbook’ appendix table 3, last row.

The article “Students Increasingly Turn to Credit Cards” (San Luis Obispo Tribune, July 21, 2006) reported that 37% of college freshmen carry a credit card balance from month to month. Suppose that the reported percentage were based on a random sample of 1,000 college freshmen.

a) Construct a 90% confidence interval for the proportion of college freshmen who carry a credit card balance from month to month.

  • Sample size (freshmen), n = 1000, \(\hat{p}\) = 0.37.

  • Confidence level: 90%

  • Before check if the random sample is large: \(n\hat{p} \geq 10\) and \(n(1 - \hat{p}) \geq 10\).

  • Formula: \(\hat{p} \pm (Z_{critical})\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p})}{n}}\)

  • \(0.37 \pm (1.645)\sqrt{\frac{0.37(1 - 0.37)}{1000}}\)

  • Lower bound: \(0.37 - (1.645)\sqrt{\frac{0.37(1 - 0.37)}{1000}}\) = 0.345

  • Upper bound: \(0.37 + (1.645)\sqrt{\frac{0.37(1 - 0.37)}{1000}}\) = 0.395

  • 90% confidence interval: (0.345, 0.395)

  • Interpretation: I am 90% confident that the proportion of ALL college freshmen that carry a credit card balance from month to month is between 34.5% and 39.5%

Confidence Interval for a Population Mean

The general formula for a confidence interval for a population mean, \(\mu\), when

  • The sample mean, \(\bar{X}\), is from a simple random sample.

  • The sample size, n, is large (generally \(\geq\) 30), and

  • The population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is KNOWN

Formula: \(\bar{X} \pm (Z_{critical})(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}})\)

This formula is still valid when n is small IF it is reasonable to believe that the distribution of values in the population is NORMAL.

Properties of the T Distribution

  • Each T curve is bell-shaped and centered at 0.

  • Each T curve is more spread out than the standard normal (z) curve.

  • As the degrees of freedom, \(\nu\), increases, the spread of the T curve decreases.

  • As \(\nu \to \infty\), the sequence of T curves approaches the standard normal curve, so the Z curve is often called the T curve with df = \(\infty\)

  • If \(\bar{X}\) and S are the mean and standard deviation of a random sample from a normal population distribution THEN The probability distribution of the standardized variable

  • \(t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}}\) is the T distribution with df = n - 1.

The One-Sample T Confidence Interval for \(\mu\)

The general formula for a confidence interval for a population \(\mu\) based a sample of size n when

  • \(\bar{X}\) is the sample mean from a simple random sample.

  • The population distribution is “normal,” OR the sample size “n” is large (generally n \(\geq\) 30), and

  • \(\sigma\), the population standard deviation is UNKNOWN

Formula: \(\bar{X} \pm (T_{critical})(\frac{S}{\sqrt{n}})\)

The T critical value is based on df = n-1. Appendix Table 3 gives critical values appropriate for each of the confidence levels 90%, 95% and 99%, as well as several other less frequently used confidence levels.

A recent Gallup poll asked 1006 Americans, “During the past year, about how many books, either hardcover or paperback, did you read either all or part of the way through?” Results of the survey indicated that \(\bar{X}\) = 13.4 books and S = 16.6 books. Construct a 99% confidence interval for the mean number of books that Americans read either all or part of during the preceding year.

  • The formula for confidence interval is \(\bar{X} \pm (t_{critical})\frac{S}{\sqrt{n}}\)

  • Given that n is large (at least 30) AND the confidence level is “99%”, the t-critical must be 2.58.

  • 13.4 \(\pm\) (2.58) \(\frac{16.6}{\sqrt{1006}}\)

  • 13.4 \(\pm\) 1.35

  • Lower bound = 13.4 - 1.35 = 12.05 and Upper bound = 13.4 + 1.35 = 14.75

  • 99% confidence interval is (12.05, 14.75)
