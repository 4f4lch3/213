The Normal Distribution

The Normal Density Function

  • The Normal Density is symmetric about the mean, \(\mu\). Since the mean is located at the center of the curve, it divides the area under the curve in half.

  • Because the mean = median = mode, the curve has a single peak and the highest point occurs at x = \(\mu\).

  • It has an inflection point at one standard deviation, \(\sigma\), to the mean. The area under the curve is 1, and the tails extend to infinity, but they never cross the horizontal axis (asymptote).

  • The area under the curve is interpreted as proportion (probability or percentage). The Normal Distribution with \(\mu\) = 0 and \(\sigma\) = 1 is known as the Standard Normal Distribution.

Standardizing a Normal Random Variable, X

Suppose that the random variable X is normally distributed with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\). Then the random variable:

  • \(Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\) is normally distributed with mean \(\mu\) = 0 and standard deviation \(\sigma\) = 1.

  • The random variable Z is said to have a Standard Normal Distribution.

  • The Standard Normal distribution has \(\mu\) = 0 and \(\sigma\) = 1.

Consider babies born in the normal range of 37-43 weeks gestational age. The paper referenced for this exercise (Fetal Growth Parameter and Birth Weight: Their Relationship to Neonatal Body Composition, Ultrasound in Obstetrics and Gynecology [2009]: 441-446) suggests that a normal distribution with mean \(\mu\) = 3500 grams and standard deviation \(\sigma\) = 600 grams is a reasonable model for the probability distribution of the continuous numerical variable X = birth weight of a randomly selected full term baby.

a) What is the probability that the birth weight of a randomly selected full-term baby exceeds 4000 grams?

  • X is Normal with \(\mu\) = 3500 and \(\sigma\) = 600

  • Find P(X > 4000)

  • Standardize X, Z = \(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\) = \(\frac{4000 - 3500}{600}\) = 0.833

  • P(X > 4000 ) = P(Z > 0.833)

  • Using table 2, round Z to two decimals, P(Z > 0.83) = 1 - P(Z < 0.83) = 1 - 0.7967

  • P(X > 4000) = 0.2033

b) What is the probability that the birth weight of a randomly selected full-term baby is between 3000 and 4000 g?

  • Find P(3000 < X < 4000)

  • P(3000 < X < 4000) = P(X < 4000) - P(X < 3000)

  • P(X < 4000) = P(Z < 0.83) = 0.7967

  • P(X < 3000) = P(Z < -0.83) = 0.2033

  • P(3000 < X < 4000) = 0.7967 - 0.2033 = 0.5934

c) What is the probability that the birth weight of a randomly selected full-term baby is either less than 2000 g or greater than 5000 g?

  • Find P(X < 2000) + P(X > 5000)

  • P(X > 5000) = 1 - P(X < 5000) = 1 - P(Z < 2.5) = 1 - 0.9938 = 0.0062

  • P(X < 2000) = P(Z < -2.5) = 0.0062

  • P(X < 2000) + P(X > 5000) = 0.0062 + 0.0062 = 0.0124

d) What is the probability that the birth weight of a randomly selected full-term baby exceeds 7 pounds? (Hint: 1 lb = 453.59 g)

  • Step 1: Change pounds to grams, 7 lb = 7*453.59 = 3175.13

  • Find P(X > 3175.13)

  • P(X > 3175.13) = 1 - P(X < 3175.13) = 1 - P(Z < -0.54)

  • P(X > 3175.13) = 1 - 0.2946 = 0.7054

e) How would you characterize the most extreme 0.1% of all full-term baby birth weights?

  • Find q such that P(X > q) = 0.001

  • For the same q, P(X < q) = 0.999

  • P(X < q) = P(Z < \(\frac{q - 3500}{600}\)) = 0.999

  • In table 2, the corresponding Z is 3.62

  • Solve for q, \(\frac{q - 3500}{600}\) = 3.62

  • q = (3.62)(600) + 3500

  • q = 5672 grams

  • In pounds, 5672/453.59 = 12.50

The Normal Probability Plot to Assess Normality

  • A normal probability plot is a graph that compares observed data versus normal scores.

  • A normal score is the expected z-score of the data value, assuming that the distribution of the random variable is normal. The values of the normal scores depend on the number of observations in the data set.

  • When the sample has been obtained from some normal population distribution, the plotted points should be close to some straight line.

  • On the other hand, systematic departure from a straight-line pattern (such as curvature in the plot) indicates that it is not reasonable to assume that the population distribution is normal.

Using the Normal distribution to Approximate a Discrete Distribution

Let X be a binomial random variable based on n trials and success with probability p, so that

  • E[X] = np

  • Var(X) = np(1 - p)

  • If np \(\geq\) 10 and n(1 - p) \(\geq\) 10 then the distribution of X is approximately normal.

  • The probability, P(a \(\leq\) X \(\leq\) b), that X is between a and b inclusive is approximately the area under the approximating normal curve between (a - 0.5) and (b + 0.5).

Suppose that 65% of all registered voters in a certain area favor a 7-day waiting period before purchase of a handgun. Among 225 randomly selected registered voters,

a) what is the probability that at least 150 favor such a waiting period?

  • X, number of voters that favor the waiting period, is a binomial random variable.

  • Before to approximate to the normal distribution, check the conditions, np \(\geq\) 10 and n(1 - p) \(\geq\) 10.

  • Parameters of X, n = 225 and p = 0.65

  • np = (225)(0.65) = 146.25; n(1 - p) = (225)(1 - 0.65) = 78.75

  • It is possible the approximation of the binomial to the normal distribution.

  • The 150 is included, so during the approximation we guarantee the inclusion by considering 149.5.

  • P(X \(\geq\) 150) \(\approx\) \(P(\hat{X} > 149.5)\)

  • The \(\hat{X}\) is normal with \(\mu\) = np and \(\sigma = \sqrt{np(1 - p)}\)

  • In order to use the z-tables, we need to standardize (find the z score) \(\hat{X}\) = 149.5.

  • \(\mu\) = np = (225)(0.65) = 146.25 and \(\sigma\) = \(\sqrt{np(1 - p)}\) = \(\sqrt{(225)(0.65)(0.35)}\) = 7.155

  • Z = \(\frac{149.5 - 146.25}{7.155}\) = 0.45

  • \(P(\hat{X} > 149.5)\) = P(Z > 0.45) = P(Z < -0.45)

  • P(X \(\geq\) 150) \(\approx\) \(P(\hat{X} > 149.5)\) = 0.3264

b) More than 150 favor such a waiting period?

  • This probability is NOT inclusive, but P(X > 150) = P(X \(\geq\) 151) because of the discreteness of X.

  • P(X > 150) = P(X \(\geq\) 151) \(\approx\) \(P(\hat{X} > 150.5)\)

  • Z = \(\frac{150.5 - 146.25}{7.155}\) = 0.59

  • \(P(\hat{X} > 150)\) = P(Z > 0.59) = P(Z < -0.59) = 0.2776

c) Between 150 and 200 inclusive favor such waiting period?

  • Find P(150 \(\leq\) X \(\leq\) 200)

  • P(150 \(\leq\) X \(\leq\) 200) \(\approx\) P(149.5 < \(\hat{X}\) < 200.5)

  • \(Z_1\) = \(\frac{149.5 - 146.25}{7.155}\) = 0.45

  • \(Z_2\) = \(\frac{200.5 - 146.25}{7.155}\) = 7.58

  • P(149.5 < \(\hat{X}\) < 200.5) = P(0.45 < Z < 7.58)

  • P(0.45 < Z < 7.58) = P(Z < 7.58) - P(Z < 0.45) = 1 - 0.6736 = 0.3264

  • P(150 \(\leq\) X \(\leq\) 200) \(\approx\) 0.3264
