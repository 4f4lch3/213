Chance Experiments and Events

  • Chance Experiment: Any activity or situation in which there is uncertainty about which two or more possible outcomes will result. For example, rolling a fair die once or tossing a fair coin twice.

  • Sample Space, S: The collection of ALL possible outcomes of a chance experiment is the sample space for the experiment.

  • Experiment 1: Roll a fair die once. S = {1,2,3,4,5,6}

  • Experiment 2: Flip a fair coin twice. S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}

  • Events: An event is ANY collection of outcomes from the sample space. A simple event is an event consisting of exactly one outcome.

  • In experiment 1, E is the event of even outcomes. E={2,4,6}

  • In experiment 2, F is the event of single tail outcomes. F = {HT, TH}

Basic Sets Operations

Let A and B denote two events:

  • Not A: The event that consists of all experimental outcomes that are not in event A. Not A is sometimes called the complement of A and is usually denoted by \(A^C\), A’, or \(\hat{A}\). (In my notes, I will use the notation A’)

  • A or B: The event that consists of all experimental outcomes that are in at least one of the two events, that is, in A or in B or in both of these. A or B is called the union of the two events and is denoted by \((A \cup B)\).

  • A and B: The event that consists of all experimental outcomes that are in both of the events A and B. A and B is called the intersection of the two events and is denoted by \((A \cap B)\).

  • \(E' \cap F'\) = {4}

  • \(E \cup F\) and \(E' \cap F'\) mutually exclusive because they do not have elements in common.

Definition of Probability

Classical Approach: When the outcomes in the sample space of a chance experiment are equally likely, the probability of an event E, denoted by P(E), is the ratio of the number of outcomes favorable to E to the total number of outcomes in the sample space:

  • P(E) = (number of outcomes favorable to E)/(number of outcomes in the sample space)

Relative Frequency Approach: The probability of an event E, denoted by P(E), is defined to be the value approached by the relative frequency of occurrence of E when a chance experiment is performed many times. If the number of times the chance experiment is performed is quite large,

  • P(E) = (number of times E occurs)/(number of times the experiment is performed)

Law of Large Numbers: As the number of repetitions of a chance experiment increases, the chance that the relative frequency of occurrence for an event will differ from the true prob- ability of the event by more than any small number approaches 0.

Probability: Basic Concepts

Probability is a numerical measure of the likelihood that a specific event will occur.

  • The probability can take any value from zero to one. The probability of zero means “impossible,” and one means “certain.”

  • The probability of the sample space, P(S), is always 1.

  • For any event E, the probability of E is denoted as P(E), and \(0 \leq P(E) \leq 1\).

  • The probability of any event, E can be understood to be the value approached by the relative frequency of occurrence of E in a very long series of trials of a chance experiment. In other words, the probability can be interpreted as a long run proportion.

Fundamental Probability Properties

  • The complement of any event E is denoted by E’.

  • The complement, E’, contains all elements (or outcomes) in S that are NOT in E.

  • By definition, P(E’) = 1 - P(E)

  • General Addition Rule: For any events E and F,

  • P(E \(\cup\) F) = P(E) + P(F) - P(E \(\cap\) F)

  • If E and F are mutually exclusive (or disjoint) then E and F DO NOT have elements (or outcomes in common). In this case, P(E \(\cap\) F) = 0

  • p + 2p + p + 2p + p + + 2p = 1.

  • 9p = 1
